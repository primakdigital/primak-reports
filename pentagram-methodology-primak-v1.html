Part 1 — Reference Case

OpenView: How Natasha Jen repositioned a VC firm nobody remembered

Client
OpenView — Boston-based VC specializing in expansion-stage software companies.
Challenge
VC branding is "typically over-polished, graphically conservative." OpenView looked like every other fund. Investors, founders, and portfolio companies couldn't explain what made OpenView different.
Team
Partner Natasha Jen, designers Jang Hyun Han and Jenny Hung.
Scope
Full rebrand: positioning, verbal identity, visual system, naming architecture, environmental graphics, digital.

What Pentagram actually did — in 6 phases:

1

Discovery

Weeks 1-3 · Research, not design

Leadership interviews — asked every partner: "What does OpenView do that no other VC does?" Found that every partner gave a different answer. The identity problem was internal before it was external.

Employee research — asked team members the same question. Found one consistent thread everyone agreed on: OpenView is hands-on. They don't just fund — they embed operators.

Competitive audit — mapped 20+ VC visual identities. Found near-identical patterns: blue/grey, serif fonts, abstract geometric marks. OpenView was indistinguishable.

Key output: The real differentiator isn't money — it's mentorship. Every partner operates inside portfolio companies. That's the brand.

2

Challenge Definition

Week 3 · One sentence that frames everything

Pentagram doesn't start with "redesign the logo." They start with: what is the one sentence that captures the gap between what this brand IS and what the market THINKS it is?

For OpenView: "OpenView operates like a hands-on partner inside every portfolio company — but their brand looks like every other check-writing VC."

The challenge became: make the hands-on operational identity visible in every brand touchpoint.

3

Brand Principles

Weeks 4-5 · Strategic foundation before any design

From discovery, Jen extracted 5 brand attributes that every design decision must embody:

Constructive · Agile · Honest · Rigorous · Personable

These aren't aspirational — they're descriptive. Found in the interviews, validated by behavior. Every design choice gets tested: "Is this constructive? Is this honest?"

Critical decision: no attribute says "prestigious," "elite," or "exclusive." OpenView's brand rejects VC vanity. The principles enforce it.

4

System Design

Weeks 5-10 · Verbal + Visual + Naming architecture

Custom typeface: OpenView Stencil — a bespoke stencil font that evokes pragmatism, efficiency, construction. Not elegant — operational.

Color: Deep blue (authority) + bright secondary palette (energy, not corporate).

Naming architecture: Replaced generic sub-brand names with action verbs: OpenView Invest, OpenView Grow, OpenView Learn. The verbs say what happens — not what the department is called.

Custom icons: Each icon represents a company characteristic or process stage — not decoration, but information.

Critical decision: the stencil typeface. It's polarizing. Some people find it ugly. Jen chose it because it can't be confused with any other VC. Distinctiveness > beauty.

5

Implementation

Weeks 10-16 · Every touchpoint

Website, pitch decks, portfolio communications, event materials, Boston office environmental graphics (including neon signage), social media templates, email signatures.

The "compliance = path of least resistance" principle: templates designed so the default output is on-brand. The team doesn't have to try to be consistent — the system makes inconsistency harder than consistency.

6

Validation

Post-launch · Did it work?

Internal test: Ask every partner again: "What does OpenView do?" After rebrand — every partner gave the same answer.

External test: Portfolio founders could now explain OpenView's value proposition to other founders in one sentence.

Market test: OpenView became visually recognizable in a category where every brand looked identical.

Part 2 — Why It Worked

Three critical decisions made the difference

Decision 1: They started with internal alignment, not external positioning.
Every partner gave a different answer to "what makes us different." The rebrand started by creating ONE answer everyone agreed on. Without this — any external message is built on sand.
Decision 2: They found the differentiator in behavior, not aspiration.
"Hands-on mentorship" wasn't invented in a workshop. It was ALREADY happening. Pentagram's job was to make the invisible visible — not to invent a new story. The strongest brand messages describe what already exists.
Decision 3: They chose distinctiveness AND beauty — not one over the other.
Jen's OpenView used a polarizing stencil font — memorable but not beautiful. That's one path. But the strongest premium brands (Moog, Leica, Braun) prove the dichotomy is false: every touchpoint is both instantly recognizable AND impossible to let go of. For a premium brand built to scale, "ugly but memorable" is not the play. The standard is: distinctive enough to never be confused, beautiful enough to never be questioned.
Natasha Jen

Phase 1 (cont.) — Discovery: Existing Business Models

Six models that match Yaniv's profile — and where each falls short

Pentagram has worked with hundreds of professional services firms. These are the six established business models closest to what Primak does. None is an exact match — that's the opportunity.

1
The Smokejumper — Rich Mironov

What it is: One-person firm. Parachutes into companies as interim VP Product/CPO. 120+ clients, 15 interim placements. Authority built on one book ("The Art of Product Management") and decades of blog posts.

Pricing: $250-400/hr. Advisory + coaching.

Where it falls short for Primak: Mironov advises and coaches — he doesn't build. Yaniv executes: UX, product architecture, business model embedding. Mironov tells you what to do. Yaniv does it.

mironov.com

2
The Operating Partner — VC Model

What it is: VC/PE firms hire senior operators who embed inside portfolio companies. Fix product-market fit, build teams, optimize go-to-market. Structurally identical to what Yaniv does for "founders and VC victims."

Pricing: Salary + carry (equity). Typically $300-500K/yr + upside.

Where it falls short for Primak: Operating Partners are employed by the fund, not independent. Yaniv does the same mechanics — enters, fixes, exits — but as an independent. The value prop is identical, the business model is different.

Common at Andreessen Horowitz, OpenView, Bessemer

3
The Product Transformation — Marty Cagan / SVPG

What it is: Team of ~8 partners, all ex-VP Product from top companies. Sell the "Product Operating Model" — teach organizations to work differently. Three books (Inspired, Empowered, Transformed). Absolute authority in the field.

Pricing: $15-50K/workshop. Corporate transformation engagements at $100K+.

Where it falls short for Primak: SVPG teaches and coaches — they never touch the product. Yaniv builds the product. SVPG changes how organizations think. Yaniv changes what organizations ship.

svpg.com

4
The Turnaround Manager — Deloitte / FTI Consulting

What it is: $50B+ global industry. Interim executives parachute into broken companies. Fix operations, finance, strategy. Restructure. Leave when it works. The exact same pattern Yaniv follows — but for finance and operations, not product.

Pricing: $500-1,500/hr. Premium, non-negotiable. The "turnaround" label justifies it.

Where it falls short for Primak: They fix balance sheets and org charts. They don't understand product, UX, or technology. Nobody does turnaround for product. This is the white space.

Deloitte, FTI Consulting, AlixPartners, Alvarez & Marsal

5
The Thought Leader — Gibson Biddle / Reforge

What it is: Ex-Netflix VP Product. 30K+ newsletter subscribers. Workshops, keynotes, courses. Monetizes expertise through education and content, not execution. Reforge's EIR program gives product leaders 6-12 month residencies to build authority.

Pricing: $10-25K/keynote. $5-15K/workshop. Courses at scale.

Where it falls short for Primak: Biddle sells knowledge. Yaniv sells results. But the authority-building mechanics — publishing, speaking, frameworks — are what Primak needs to adopt for visibility.

gibsonbiddle.com · reforge.com

6
The Experience Studio — McKinsey Digital

What it is: Global network of immersive learning spaces. Business leaders experience digital transformation firsthand: rapid prototyping, design thinking, AI interaction, VR/AR. 3-day to 2-week programs. Not a lecture — an experience where leaders see and feel the change before committing.

Pricing: $50-500K per engagement (bundled with McKinsey consulting).

Where it falls short for Primak: Requires massive infrastructure and brand. But the principle is relevant: show, don't tell. The Diagnose phase in Primak's model should function like a mini Experience Studio — let the client experience the problem and the solution, not just hear about it.

mckinsey.com/experience-studio

Phase 2 — Challenge Definition

The one-sentence challenge

"Yaniv turns stuck technology into working product across every domain he enters — but his brand sounds like one of 3,000 Fractional CPOs, none of his proof is published, and the market can't find him."

Three sub-challenges:

Identity gap — The brand says "Fractional CPO." The work says "problem solver who transforms technology into product."

Visibility gap — Zero indexed content connects Yaniv to any of his 5 cases. Complete online invisibility.

Differentiation gap — The category exists: Interim/Embedded CPO (Harpal Singh, Mironov, Prodify, CXO Partners). But Yaniv's differentiators aren't articulated: cross-domain (5 industries, 0 failures), $585M outcome, and craft + strategy + execution depth that most interim CPOs don't touch.

Phase 3 — Brand Principles

Five attributes found in Yaniv's behavior

Not aspirational. Found in the 5 case studies and in how Yaniv described his work during this session.

Direct
"מהבטן." No frameworks for the sake of frameworks. Simplifies, then executes. Said explicitly: "I'm not a designer — simplify / execute."
Versatile
Medical, cyber, media, hospitality, B2B2C. The skill transfers because it's not domain knowledge — it's the ability to see what's broken and build what's missing.
Relentless
5 companies. 0 failures. Doesn't leave until it works. CathWorks: from "doctors don't trust it" to FDA cleared, $585M. Orchestra: from nothing to Cisco partnership.
Founder-native
"Founders and VC victims." Speaks founder language, not consultant language. Understands the pressure of money, time, and board expectations from the inside.
Commercial
"Embed business models into product and improve ROI." Not just a craft leader — builds products that make money. The output is revenue, not wireframes.
The Test
OpenView method
Every brand decision from here gets filtered: "Is this direct? Is this versatile? Is this relentless? Is this founder-native? Is this commercial?" If not — it doesn't belong in Primak's brand.
Phase 4 — System Design: The Six Components

What needs to change — and to what

1
Positioning

Assumption validated in session: Yaniv confirmed "I turn technology into product" as accurate.

Current
"Fractional CPO for high-stakes AI. We ship the products humans need to trust."

Category: Fractional executive
Market: AI only
Promise: trust
Proposed
"I turn stuck technology into product."

Category: Product problem solver (new)
Market: Any company with resources but no working product
Promise: from stuck to working

AI as primary proof vertical, not a cage.

Open question: Should AI be in the one-liner or only in the proof cases? Data says AI = premium + zero competition. Yaniv's instinct says broader. The principles say: lead with what's TRUE (versatile), use AI as the sharpest proof.

2
Messaging System

OpenView used action verbs (Invest, Grow, Learn). Primak needs a similar verb-driven system.

Current
One one-liner used everywhere. No system. No adaptation per context.
Proposed
Core message: "Turns stuck technology into product."

Proof message (conversations): "5 companies, 5 industries, zero failures. From AI cardiac ($585M exit) to national newsroom transformation."

Founder message (referrals): "The guy you call when you have money and tech but no product — and nobody knows why."

Three messages. Each has one job.
3
Value Proposition
Current
Implicit: "I provide part-time CPO services."
Frame: time-for-money
Result: price comparison with other fractional CPOs ($3-15K/mo range)
Proposed
"You have the technology, the funding, and the team — but no product that works. I come in, find what's stuck, build what's missing, and leave when it's running."

Frame: outcome-for-investment
Result: no price comparison possible — nobody else does this
4
Service Architecture

OpenView used action verbs. Primak services should describe what happens, not what the package is called.

Current
Deferred. Direction was 3 branded packages (Scan/Sprint/Residency). Generic consulting language.
Proposed
One offering: "Product Intervention by Primak"

Three scopes, described by what happens:
· Diagnose — 2-3 weeks. Find what's stuck and why. [$15K]
· Build — 6-12 weeks. Fix it, ship it. [$22K+]
· Lead — ongoing. Stay until the function runs without me. [$13K/mo]

Diagnose is the paid entry gate. Free diagnosis = devalued thinking (Dunford/Enns principle).
5
Proof System

This is where the biggest gap exists. All proof is currently private. Zero is indexed.

Current
5 cases exist in proposals and conversations. Zero published. Zero indexed. Zero findable by AI search.
Proposed
5 elegantly written case studies. Paula Scher's format — state facts, never claim credit:

"AI cardiac diagnostics. UX strategy from concept through FDA clearance. 100K patients. Acquired by Medtronic, $585M."

Each published as: LinkedIn article + website page + SEO-optimized vertical landing page.

The "5/5/0" proof line for conversations: "5 companies. 5 industries. Zero failures."
6
Title & Identity
Current
Founder & CPO, Primak

"Fractional CPO" in one-liner
Proposed
CPO, Primak — everywhere.

"Fractional CPO" appears only in LinkedIn About/Experience for search indexing. Never in headline, never in one-liner, never in conversation.

The OpenView principle: the title describes the function, not the arrangement. OpenView didn't call themselves "part-time mentors" — they just mentored.

Phase 6 — Validation

How we know it worked

The OpenView test: can someone who isn't you explain what you do in one sentence?

Test 1 — Referral Test (Week 4)

Ask 3 people who know your work: "If someone asked you what I do, what would you say?" If they say something close to "he turns stuck technology into product" — the messaging works. If they say "he's a freelance designer" or "he does UX" — it doesn't.

Test 2 — Search Test (Week 6)

Search "CathWorks product leader" and "fractional CPO Israel" — does Yaniv Primak appear? If yes after 5 case studies + vertical pages — the SEO works.

Test 3 — Inbound Test (Week 8)

Did anyone reach out based on a published case study or LinkedIn post? One inbound lead from content = the system is working. Zero = content needs adjustment.

Test 4 — Price Test (Ongoing)

Does the new positioning allow charging ₪450+ without negotiation? If prospects discuss scope rather than rate — the value framing works.
