Part 1 — Analysis of Current CV

What works, what doesn't

What works

+
Profile paragraph is strong. "I turn stuck technology into working product" — already aligned with new positioning. The pattern (funding + talent, no product) is clear.
+
CathWorks entry is powerful. FDA clearance, 100K patients, $585M acquisition — real numbers, verifiable outcomes. This is the strongest entry in the CV.
+
Domain breadth is visible. Healthcare, cybersecurity, media, B2B — the versatility is present. "Five domains, zero failures" is there.

What doesn't work

!
Reads like a freelancer portfolio, not an executive CV. 15 entries across two categories. Decision-makers scanning this see quantity, not authority. A CPO's CV should show 5-7 significant engagements, not every project.
!
Organized by domain, not by impact. Healthcare gets 6 entries — but only CathWorks is a major engagement. The small projects (NeuroBrave, ClubD, NGT, TAU) dilute the signal. A reader can't distinguish a $585M engagement from a branding gig.
!
Role titles undermine the positioning. "Clinical Interface Design," "Brand & Design," "Visual Communication," "Design Lead" — these position Yaniv as a designer, not a product leader. Directly contradicts the new identity.
!
No outcomes except CathWorks. Every other entry describes WHAT the project is, not WHAT HAPPENED because Yaniv was there. No revenue impact, no scale metrics, no before/after.
!
Haaretz is buried. 3-year transformation, 70 people, Head of Product — this is a top-3 career entry and it sits mid-page under "Technology, Scale & Transformation" with no prominence.
!
Orchestra is under-described. "Product Design" title. No mention that they had been struggling for nearly a year before Yaniv joined. No mention of building from zero. The Cisco partnership reads as a footnote.
!
Selina is missing entirely. One of the 5 proof cases (unicorn, hospitality, mobile acquisition fix) is absent from the CV.
!
"Additional" section is an afterthought. Teaching at Open University + Techstars at the bottom, unlabeled, conveys zero strategic value.
!
No proof architecture. The "5 domains, zero failures" line is in the profile but nowhere else. No system for making outcomes scannable. No visual hierarchy between major and minor engagements.
Core problem: this CV describes projects. It should demonstrate a pattern. The pattern is: stuck technology → Yaniv enters → working product. Every entry should show that arc — or be cut.

Part 3 — Design Direction

How this CV should look and feel

Creative direction for implementation. Not graphic execution — strategic guidance for the final branded document.

1. Design Concept

Direction: Executive Editorial.

The CV should feel like an executive brief, not a creative portfolio. Think McKinsey partner biography meets Monocle editorial. Clean, authoritative, information-dense but never crowded. The design communicates: "this person operates at the executive level" before a single word is read.

Reference mood: Moog product sheets (precision + beauty), Pentagram case study pages (clean grid, clear hierarchy), The Economist typography (authority without decoration).

NOT: creative portfolio, colorful infographic, "modern resume template," anything with icons, charts, or skill bars.

2. Layout & Hierarchy

Page 1 (above the fold — the decision page):

Name + Title + Contact → Profile (3 lines max) → Proof Bar (5 metrics, black background, white text) → Top 2-3 Key Engagements with outcome lines. Everything a decision-maker needs to say "I want to meet this person" in 7 seconds.

Page 2 (supporting depth):

Remaining Key Engagements → Healthcare Portfolio (compressed) → Education & Advisory. This page exists for due diligence — the decision was made on page 1.

Grid: Single column. No sidebar. No two-column layout. Executive documents are read top-to-bottom, not scanned left-to-right. Generous left margin (identity column for section labels, aligned left).

Whitespace: Aggressive. The emptiness IS the authority. A CV with 60% whitespace communicates seniority. A packed CV communicates anxiety.

3. Typography Direction

Hierarchy (4 levels):

Level 1 — Name: Large, heavy weight (800-900). The name should be the largest element on the page. No logo. The name IS the logo.

Level 2 — Section titles: Small caps, wide letter-spacing, light weight. "KEY ENGAGEMENTS" as a quiet structural label, not a shouted heading.

Level 3 — Entry titles: Bold, slightly larger than body. Company name is the anchor. Domain sits right-aligned as a tag.

Level 4 — Body: Regular weight, generous line height (1.6-1.7). Outcome lines in bold or medium weight, slightly smaller than body.

Typographic tone: Neo-grotesque. Clean, no serifs, no decorative elements. The typeface should disappear — letting the content speak. Inter, Graphik, Founders Grotesk, or Suisse Int'l family. One family only — no mixing.

Proof Bar numbers: Tabular figures, heavy weight. The numbers should look like they belong on a financial report, not a poster.

4. Proof Presentation System

Proof Bar: Black background strip, full width, immediately below profile. Five metrics in equal columns. Large numbers (18-24pt) over small uppercase labels (8-9pt). This is the "billboard" — the first thing eyes go to after the name.

Outcome Lines: Each Key Engagement ends with a bold line prefixed by "→". This creates a scannable pattern — a reader can skim just the arrows and get the full story: $585M... 70-person department... Cisco partnership... concept to market.

Domain Tags: Right-aligned, uppercase, light weight. MedTech AI / Cybersecurity / Digital Media. They provide context without competing with the company name. Subtle categorization that helps the reader place each engagement instantly.

Hierarchy between Key Engagements and Portfolio: Key Engagements get full entries (company, role, description, outcome). Portfolio items get one-line compressed entries (name + one-sentence description). The visual weight difference immediately communicates: these are the stories that matter.

5. Visual Language

Color: Black and white. Period. One accent color ONLY for the proof bar or outcome arrows — if needed. The Primak brand can be monochrome at this stage. Color is for the website, not the CV.

Lines: Thin horizontal rules (0.5pt, light grey) to separate sections. No boxes, no borders around entries. Lines are structural, not decorative.

Spacing: Section spacing > entry spacing > line spacing. The rhythm should feel architectural — each section is a room, each entry is furniture. Enough space to breathe, not enough to feel empty.

Icons: None. Zero. No industry icons, no contact icons, no skill icons. Icons are for junior designers. This CV speaks with words and numbers.

Elements: The ONLY graphic element is the Proof Bar (black strip with white numbers). Everything else is pure typography. The proof bar functions as both a visual anchor and an information device — this is the Moog principle: beautiful AND functional.

6. Format Variants

Primary: Executive PDF (2 pages). Full CV. Formatted for print and screen. This is the asset sent to clients, partners, investors, and board-level contacts. Must look flawless on both a 27" screen and an A4 printout.

Secondary: Word (.docx) — Branded Template. Same content and visual structure as the PDF, implemented in Word with proper heading styles, consistent spacing, and Primak branded template. Export-ready — no reformatting needed by recipient. Use the Primak Word template (primaktemp.dotx) as base.

Tertiary: LinkedIn-ready text. Plain text version optimized for copy-paste into LinkedIn's About section and Experience entries. No formatting dependencies. Same content hierarchy, adapted for LinkedIn's constraints.

One-page variant: For situations requiring a single page (conference bios, board packages): Profile + Proof Bar + 5 Key Engagements (3-line each) + Education (one line). Cut the portfolio section entirely.

Do

  • Precision: Every word earns its place. If it doesn't advance the narrative, cut it.
  • Authority: Whitespace, clean typography, large name. The document should feel senior before reading starts.
  • Clarity: 7-second scan reveals: who, what domains, what outcomes, what scale.
  • Outcomes first: The proof bar is the first visual element after the name. Numbers precede narratives.
  • Pattern recognition: Reader should feel the "enters stuck → leaves working" arc across all entries.
  • Print-ready: Must look as good printed on paper as it does on screen.

Don't

  • No icons. No skill bars. No charts. No infographics. No rating systems.
  • No color. Black, white, one possible accent. That's it.
  • No decoration. No borders, shadows, gradients, background textures.
  • No fluff. No "passionate about innovation." No "proven track record of excellence." Let the numbers talk.
  • No portfolio aesthetics. This is not a creative portfolio. No full-bleed images, no project thumbnails, no mood boards.
  • No "modern CV template" look. No sidebar with skills, no headshot circle, no progress bars.
  • No equal weight. If CathWorks ($585M) and ClubD (branding gig) look the same — the hierarchy failed.
Implementation — Word Document

How to build this in Word

Template

Use primaktemp.dotx as base. Override styles as needed to match the design direction above.

Heading Styles

Heading 1: Name — 24-28pt, weight 800, no spacing above, 6pt below
Heading 2: Section titles — 9pt, uppercase, letter-spacing 3pt, color #888, 24pt above, 12pt below
Heading 3: Company names — 13pt, weight 700, 16pt above, 2pt below
Normal: Body — 10.5pt, weight 400, line spacing 1.5, color #444
Outcome: Custom style — 10pt, weight 600, color #111, 4pt above

Proof Bar in Word

Implement as a 5-column table with black cell shading, white text, no borders. Row height: 1.2cm. Center-aligned. This is the one "graphic" element — it breaks the monotony and anchors the page.

Page Setup

A4 · Margins: Top 2.5cm, Bottom 2cm, Left 3cm, Right 2.5cm · Left margin is intentionally larger — creates a clean reading column. No headers/footers on page 1. Page 2: subtle footer with "Yaniv Primak — CPO, Primak" right-aligned, 8pt, color #ccc.
