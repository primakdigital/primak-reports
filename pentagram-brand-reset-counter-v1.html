03 — Decision by Decision

Where Team A went wrong — and what we'd do instead

1
One-Liner

Team A split it into 3 assets. We say: one line or nothing.

The Problem with Team A

"Product leadership for AI that can't fail" is built on fear. "Can't fail" is a negative. Nobody wakes up excited to hire the person who prevents failure — they hire the person who creates success. Fear sells insurance. Ambition sells partnerships. And "Product leadership" is a category description, not a brand. That's like Porsche saying "Automotive engineering for roads that are fast."

Domenic Lippa
Naming
"Also — three assets for one introduction? A brand line, a positioning statement, AND a proof line? Yaniv has ADHD. His clients are busy CEOs. This system will collapse after the first week. One line. One thing. Memorizable by anyone who hears it once."

Our Counter

One line that does everything — positions, differentiates, and sticks. No system required.

Counter-Proposal — Recommended
Primak builds the AI products that need to get it right the first time.
15 words. "Builds" = active, constructive (not "leads" or "manages"). "AI products" = category. "Need to get it right the first time" = implies stakes WITHOUT fear. It's about precision, not avoidance. "First time" implies efficiency and confidence — not anxiety. And "Primak" is the subject — the name IS the brand.
Counter-Proposal — Alternative
The product mind behind $585M in AI exits. Now available.
Proof-first. The number does everything — credibility, scale, stakes. "Now available" is deliberately casual — chill authority. Implies scarcity. Risk: ties identity to one exit forever.
Counter-Proposal — Radical
Primak. Your AI deserves a CPO.
6 words. Reframes the conversation entirely: the question isn't "do I need a fractional CPO" — it's "does my AI product deserve real product leadership?" The answer is always yes. Shortest possible distance between hearing it and wanting a meeting.
2
Title

Team A created two identities. We say: that's schizophrenia.

The Problem with Team A

A "dual system" where LinkedIn says one thing and your business card says another. What happens when a prospect sees your LinkedIn, then gets your proposal, and the title is different? That's not a system — that's inconsistency disguised as strategy. And putting "$585M Medtronic" in a LinkedIn headline is the opposite of chill. It's a billboard on your forehead.

Michael Bierut
Lead Partner
"Mastercard didn't have one logo for business cards and another for digital. We made ONE mark that works everywhere. That's the job. If your title doesn't work in every context, it's not a title — it's a costume change."

Our Counter

Counter-Proposal — One Title Everywhere
CPO, Primak
Two words. Works on LinkedIn, business cards, email signatures, proposals, introductions. "CPO" = what you do. "Primak" = who you are. No "Founder" (ego). No "Fractional" (ceiling). No "$585M" (billboard). The credibility comes from the CONVERSATION, not the headline. Let the title open the door — the track record walks through it.
Eddie Opara

Services

Team A branded 3 products. We say: you're not a menu — you're a chef.

The Problem with Team A

"Primak Scan. Primak Sprint. Primak Residency." This is consulting-firm cosplay. McKinsey has named products because they have 45,000 consultants delivering them at scale. Primak is Yaniv. The product IS Yaniv. Packaging his time into branded tiers creates the illusion of scale where the value is actually intimacy. A CEO doesn't want to buy a "Scan" — they want to talk to the person who shipped CathWorks.

4
Proof Cases

Team A built a matrix. We say: matrices feel rehearsed.

The Problem with Team A

A "Proof Matrix" with signals, cases, and hooks. It's smart operationally — but if a prospect senses Yaniv is running a playbook, they lose trust immediately. The best proof is the one that feels spontaneous — like Yaniv just happened to mention the most relevant thing. Systems are for CRM notes, not for human conversation.

Our Counter

Counter-Proposal
One universal story. Five endings.
Start every proof the same way: "I've built AI products across medical devices, cybersecurity, and media." One sentence, three worlds. Then let the prospect's REACTION guide which story you go deeper on. They lean forward at "medical devices" → CathWorks. They ask about "cybersecurity" → Orchestra. The prospect picks the case. You just tell the story they asked for. Feels spontaneous. Is actually systematic.
Michael Bierut
Lead Partner
"The Saks identity works because it's one idea — a grid — with infinite expressions. Same here. One opening line, infinite endings. The system is invisible. The conversation feels natural."
5
Verticals

Team A added a third trigger. We say: two was sharper.

The Problem with Team A

The original razor: "If your AI fails, does someone get hurt or does a deal die?" Two triggers. Binary. Sharp. Team A added "or does a regulator call" — which dilutes it. Regulation is a symptom of the first two. If someone gets hurt → regulators call. If a deal dies → compliance was probably the reason. The third trigger adds a category without adding clarity.

Counter-Proposal
Keep the original razor. Two triggers. Don't touch it.
"Does someone get hurt or does a deal die?" — this is already perfect. It's binary, visceral, and self-qualifying. Adding a third option makes it a checklist instead of a gut check. Two triggers = one second to answer. Three triggers = thinking about it. You want the gut reaction, not the analysis.
Eddie Opara
Digital
"Every word you add to a filter reduces its power. The best search queries are short. The best qualifiers are binary."
6
Voice

Team A added three tests. We say: one test rules them all.

The Problem with Team A

Forward Test. Number Test. Chill Test. Three checkpoints before every piece of content. For someone with ADHD who needs to move fast, that's three reasons to skip the whole process. Tests work when there's ONE. The moment you have three, you're back to a quality manual nobody reads.

Counter-Proposal — The Only Test
"Would I send this if I had 10 other clients waiting?"
One question. Captures everything. If you'd send it with 10 clients waiting — it's sharp, it's not trying too hard, and it's worth the reader's time. If you wouldn't — it's either too long, too needy, or too weak. The scarcity mindset produces the same results as Forward + Number + Chill combined. One question. One second.
Jody H-P
Technology
"Channel 4's brand system works because we gave them principles, not checklists. Principles are flexible. Checklists break under pressure. One principle: scarcity. Everything else follows."
7
Deal Gates

Team A renamed them. We say: the client never sees these names.

The Problem with Team A

"Investment Fit" instead of "Budget Check." "Authority Map" instead of "Decision Maker ID." These names are internal tools. The client will never hear them. Renaming internal processes as "brand language" is decoration — it costs time and adds zero value. Save the naming energy for things the market actually sees.

Counter-Proposal
Keep the original names. They're operational tools, not brand assets.
Budget Check. Decision Maker ID. Scope Lock. Floor Price. Exit Trigger. These are blunt, clear, and functional. They work because Yaniv uses them internally. Rebranding internal processes is the same impulse that made corporations rename "HR" to "People & Culture" — the department didn't change, just the door sign.
Domenic Lippa
Naming
"A name earns its place when someone outside the company says it. If only Yaniv says these words, they don't need to be branded. Save the naming calories."

Team A Philosophy

Build a system. Every context gets the right tool.

Strength: flexibility, search optimization, thoroughness.
Risk: complexity. Yaniv has to remember which asset goes where. The system depends on perfect execution.

Team B Philosophy

Be one thing. Say one thing. Say it everywhere.

Strength: simplicity, consistency, zero maintenance.
Risk: less optimized for LinkedIn search. May sacrifice some findability for purity.

The real question for Yaniv: do you want a toolkit you deploy differently in each context? Or do you want a signal that's the same everywhere and lets the conversation do the rest?
