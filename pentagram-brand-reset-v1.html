02 — Discovery

What we found

We reviewed Primak's current positioning, competitive landscape, buyer simulation data, and LinkedIn presence. Here's what stood out.

Market Density
LinkedIn returns 3,000+ profiles for "Fractional CPO." It returns <50 for "AI product leadership trust safety." Primak is competing in a crowded hallway while an empty room sits next door.
Buyer Language
Buyers of product leadership don't search for "Fractional." They search for outcomes: "AI product strategy," "CPO for hire," "product leadership for healthtech." "Fractional" is supply-side jargon describing a work arrangement, not a value proposition.
Title Conflict
"Founder & CPO" tells the market what Yaniv is. It doesn't tell them what they get. Every competitor who succeeds in this space puts the buyer's need in the headline, not the seller's ego.
AI Discovery
In 2026, a growing share of prospects discover providers through AI search — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude. These systems rank by specificity, not volume. "Fractional CPO" returns noise. "Product leader for FDA-cleared AI" returns Primak — if the positioning supports it.
One-liner Load
The current one-liner does 4 jobs in 14 words: defines role, defines market, claims capability, establishes stakes. That's 4 messages competing for one sentence. Each deserves its own space in the brand system.
03 — Core Insight

Stop selling the arrangement.
Start selling the outcome.

"Fractional" tells every prospect how much of you they get. It doesn't tell them what happens when you show up. Bloomberg doesn't introduce itself as 'partial financial data.' Your AI clients have products where failure means someone gets hurt. Lead with that."
— Paula Scher, Strategic Advisor

The reframe: use "Fractional CPO" as a search keyword (LinkedIn, SEO, AI retrieval). But never as a brand identity. The brand is about what happens when Primak walks in — not the hours-per-week arrangement.

04 — Recommendations

7 decisions, redesigned

Each recommendation comes with our reasoning. Where relevant, we show alternatives. The red badge marks our pick.

Pentagram Pick
Decision #1

One-Liner → Brand System (3 Assets)

Was: "Fractional CPO for high-stakes AI. We ship the products humans need to trust."

A single one-liner can't do everything. We split it into three purpose-built assets — each doing one job well.

Asset 1 — Brand Line

Purpose: memorable, repeatable, goes on walls and slides.

Recommended
Product leadership for AI that can't satisfail.
8 words. Drops "Fractional" (ceiling). Drops "We ship" (startup cliché). The tension is in "can't fail" — it implies stakes without listing them. The buyer fills in their own context: medical, defense, financial, whatever their world is.
Alternative A
Your AI can't fail. We make sure it doesn't.
Conversational, bold. Positions Primak as insurance. Risk: slightly aggressive for first-contact contexts.
Alternative B
AI that can't fail needs product that won't.
Elegant parallel structure. Intellectual. Risk: too clever — might not land in a 3-second scan.

Asset 2 — Positioning Statement

Purpose: searchable, descriptive. Used in bios, proposals, LinkedIn About.

Recommended
Fractional CPO for companies building AI products where failure isn't an option — from FDA-cleared medical devices to national cybersecurity platforms.
"Fractional CPO" is here for search. The specifics (FDA, cybersecurity) signal caliber. This is where the credentials live.

Asset 3 — Proof Line

Purpose: conversation starter. Drops in meetings, DMs, introductions.

Recommended
Led the product that Medtronic paid $585M for. Now I do it for AI companies where the stakes are just as high.
The $585M number does the heavy lifting. "Just as high" creates instant comparison. This is the conversation-starter — not the headline.

Decision #4

Proof Cases → Proof Matrix with Hooks

Was: Flex by audience — CathWorks (MedTech), Orchestra (Cyber), Haaretz (Scale), Queue (CPO), Selina (Startup)

"Flex by audience" is right — but needs rules. Never more than 2 cases per conversation. Each case needs a hook — the one sentence that makes the prospect lean forward.

Prospect Signal Lead Case Hook (one sentence)
Mentions FDA, regulation, compliance CathWorks "Built the AI cardiac product that got FDA clearance and led to a $585M Medtronic acquisition. 100K patients treated."
Security, defense, national infrastructure Orchestra "Product-led the cyber platform built by NSO Group founders. Orchestrated the Cisco partnership."
Scaling team, organizational complexity Haaretz "Led 70 people through a 3-year digital transformation at Israel's leading newspaper."
Doubts fractional can be "real" CPO Queue "I wasn't fractional there. I was CPO. Full title, full authority, full accountability."
Early stage, high growth, consumer Selina "Led product at a hospitality unicorn — travel, scale, speed."
Giorgia Lupi
Data & Evidence
"Don't list proof cases — deploy them. The matrix gives you a system: listen for the signal, deliver the hook. Two cases maximum per prospect. Depth beats breadth every time."
Decision #5

Verticals → Keep "High-Stakes AI" + Add a Razor

Was: "High-stakes AI" IS the vertical. Qualification: "If your AI fails, does someone get hurt or does a deal die?"

This tested 9/9 in buyer simulation. We don't fix what works. But we sharpen the tool.

Refined Qualification Razor
"If your AI fails — does someone get hurt, does a deal die, or does a regulator call?"
Added "regulator calls" — captures FinTech, InsurTech, GovTech prospects who aren't medical or cyber but still play in high-stakes. Three triggers instead of two. Broader net, same precision.
"High-stakes AI" isn't a vertical — it's a filter. It works precisely because it excludes. Every prospect who hears it immediately knows if they qualify. That self-selection is worth more than any target list.
— Andrea Trabucco-Campos
Decision #6

Voice → Add Three Operational Tests

Was: English-first. Chill authority. Numbers > adjectives. Never first person.

The principles are right. What's missing are tests — quick checks before anything goes out.

Test 1 — The Forward Test
"Would a VP Product forward this to their CEO?"
If no — rewrite. Content must be worth sharing up the chain. This is how inbound deals start: someone forwards you.
Test 2 — The Number Test
"Does every claim have a number?"
"Transformed digital operations" → NO. "Led 70-person department through 3-year digital transformation" → YES. No number = no credibility. Numbers are the voice.
Test 3 — The Chill Test
"Does this sound like proving or like someone who already has?"
Already in your system (from 19.02). Keeping it as an explicit test alongside the other two. If it sounds like proving → tone down.
Paula Scher
Strategic Advisor
"Rules without tests are decorations. You'll break them under pressure — everyone does. Tests are faster. Three seconds: forward-worthy? Has numbers? Sounds chill? Yes, yes, yes — send."
Decision #7

Deal Gates → Keep + Rename as Brand Language

Was: 5 mandatory gates: Budget Check, Decision Maker ID, Scope Lock, Floor Price, Exit Trigger

The gates are operationally sound. We're not changing the mechanics — we're giving them language that reinforces the brand.

# Was Now Why
G1 Budget Check Investment Fit "Budget check" sounds like you're the vendor. "Investment fit" frames it as mutual qualification.
G2 Decision Maker ID Authority Map Not just "who signs" — map the decision chain. Know who influences, who blocks, who signs.
G3 Scope Lock Scope Lock This one's already perfect. Clear, non-negotiable, brand-aligned. Keep it.
G4 Floor Price Rate Standard "Floor" implies you might go lower. "Standard" implies this is what it costs. ₪450/hr · $275/hr.
G5 Exit Trigger Exit Clause "Trigger" sounds reactive. "Clause" sounds pre-agreed. 6 months no data = clean exit.
