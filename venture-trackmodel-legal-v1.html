Track Model — Legal & IP Layer

v1 | 21.02.2026 | PRIMO Venture Protocol | Supplement to Go/No-Go Report
What You Can and Can't Do — Traffic Light
Track shape (geometric layout) — NOT copyrightable. Geographic fact. Free to use.
Personal GPS data from rider's own ride — Rider's data, rider's memory. Fully clear.
OpenStreetMap / public topographic data — Commercially usable (ODbL license with attribution).
Local / lesser-known circuits — Minimal enforcement. Most welcome the publicity.
Circuit names used descriptively — Possible under nominative fair use + non-affiliation disclaimer. Trackscapes + Golf Project operate this way for years.
Selling on Etsy/Amazon — One trademark complaint = instant takedown. Own site is safer.
"F1", "Formula 1", "MotoGP", "Grand Prix" — Strictly trademarked. NEVER use without license.
Circuit logos or F1/MotoGP/FIA logos — Copyright + trademark. Zero tolerance.
Claiming "officially licensed" — When you're not. Fraud.
Precedents — How Others Do It

Trackscapes (UK) — "Unofficial hobbyist supplier"

100+ circuits, uses official names, operates for years.
Disclaimer: "not affiliated or associated with any organisations."
No license. No enforcement action.

The Golf Project (US) — "Artistic representations only"

6,000+ customers, 2,500+ golf courses, $225-$420.
Disclaimer: "not affiliated with or endorsed by any golf course."
No license. No enforcement. Exact same legal model.

3D Racetracks + Nurburgring — Licensed partnership

Nurburgring approached THEM for licensing. Product sold in official paddock shop.
Proves circuits WANT licensed products. Small operators can get deals.

Linear Edge (US) — NASCAR license

Licensing agreement with NASCAR + majority of North American tracks.
Licensed status = competitive moat + retail distribution.
Licensing as strategy, not just compliance.
7 Creative Strategies — Ranked by Safety

A. Personal GPS Data Products

"Your Ride at [Location]" — model from rider's own GPS data. Their data, their ride, their memory. Zero IP risk. Maximum personalization premium.
SAFE — Phase 1 MVP

B. Local / Track Day Circuits First

Start with regional tracks (Greece, Spain, Italy track day venues). Not F1 calendar. Minimal enforcement, often welcome publicity. Build brand before touching big names.
SAFE — Phase 1

C. OpenStreetMap / Public Data Derived

Generate track shapes from OSM + public elevation data (SRTM). GitHub: TUMFTM/racetrack-database has 20+ tracks as GPS points. Your 3D interpretation = your copyright.
SAFE — Phase 1

D. "Geographic Art" — No Names

Sell shape only. Use GPS coordinates as product name ("45.6156°N, 9.2811°E"). Fans recognize the shape. Zero trademark exposure. SEO challenge.
SAFE

E. Trackscapes Model — Descriptive Use + Disclaimer

Use circuit names to describe (not brand). Non-affiliation disclaimer. Widely used, years of operation without enforcement. The industry standard for small sellers.
MODERATE — Phase 2

F. Direct Circuit Licensing

Contact circuits directly (not F1 corporate). Offer royalty + gift shop placement. Nurburgring precedent proves it works. License = moat + distribution.
GROWTH — Phase 3

G. Track Day Organizer Partnerships

Partner with organizers (Track Rabbit, MotorsportReg, local operators). They have circuit relationships. Co-branded: "Your ride at [circuit] with [organizer]".
GROWTH — Phase 2-3
Licensing Impact on Margins
Typical sports licensing royalty 8-12% of net sales
Product at $200, no license GM 73% ($110 profit/unit)
Product at $200, with 10% royalty GM 63% ($90 profit/unit)
Minimum annual guarantees (major properties) $10K — $100K+ (cash flow risk)
The counter-intuitive insight: Licensing costs money (-10% GM) but CREATES a moat. If you have exclusive circuit licenses, competitors can't legally sell those tracks. Linear Edge's NASCAR deal = competitors locked out. Licensing is an investment, not just a cost.
Legal Roadmap — Phased Approach

Phase 1: No License Needed (Month 1-6)

Personal GPS data products + local track day circuits + OSM-derived shapes.
No famous names. Build brand. Prove product-market fit.
Legal budget: $0. Consult IP attorney only before Phase 2 ($2-5K).

Phase 2: Descriptive Use (Month 6-12)

Add famous circuit shapes with descriptive naming + non-affiliation disclaimer (Trackscapes model).
Own Shopify store (not Etsy — lower takedown risk).
Start licensing conversations with 3-5 mid-tier circuits.
Legal budget: $2-5K (IP attorney consultation for international sales from Israel).

Phase 3: Licensed Player (Month 12+)

Secure circuit-level licenses for top 5-10 selling tracks.
"Only officially licensed [circuit] sculpture" = marketing differentiator + premium pricing.
Explore F1/MotoGP corporate licensing only if revenue justifies minimums.
Legal budget: $5-10K/year (royalties + legal).
Worst Case Scenarios
Cease & Desist letter — Most likely first action. Remove/rename product. Business continues. Probability: Moderate (if using famous names).
Platform takedown — Etsy/Amazon listings removed on complaint. Account strikes. Probability: Low-Moderate on platforms.
Lawsuit — Very unlikely for small seller of art products. Would require cross-border enforcement (expensive for plaintiff). Probability: Very Low.
Bottom Line
IP is NOT a blocker for Go/No-Go. The personal GPS data angle + local circuits = zero legal risk for Phase 1. Famous circuit names can be added in Phase 2 using the industry-standard disclaimer model (proven by Trackscapes + Golf Project). Long-term, licensing becomes a competitive MOAT, not just a cost.
